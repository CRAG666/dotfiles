# Exemplifying

Examples in academic writing serve three distinct functions: (1) supporting a general claim with concrete evidence, (2) clarifying an unfamiliar concept, and (3) demonstrating to the reader that the writer has grasped a complex problem. Q1 prose makes the rhetorical function of each example explicit - the reader should never have to guess why a case is being introduced.

## Examples as additional information in a sentence

Use to embed an example without breaking the flow of the main claim. *Such as*, *including*, *for example*, *e.g.* are the standard markers.

- Young people begin smoking for a variety of reasons, such as pressure from peers or ...
- The prices of base metals, such as copper, iron ore, and aluminum, have declined over ...
- Many diseases can result at least in part from stress, including arthritis, asthma, and migraine.
- Author kept in close contact with many other scholars, such as X, Y, Z, and ...
- Author found that if some other stimulus, for example the ringing of a bell, preceded the food, the ...
- Several factors - including age, sex, and prior exposure - were controlled for in the analysis.
- Standard tools (e.g., X, Y) were used to ...
- Common methods of measuring X (such as Y and Z) tend to ...

A note on punctuation: *such as* and *including* introduce a non-exhaustive list; *namely* introduces an exhaustive specification. Do not write *"e.g., ..., etc."* - *e.g.* already signals "non-exhaustive", so *etc.* is redundant.

### Pointing the reader to listed examples

Use when the examples themselves are relegated to a list, table, or appendix to keep the main text uncluttered.

- Examples are listed in the appendix.
- Examples are given in the appendix.
- Further examples are provided in the appendix (see Appendix X).
- See Appendix X for a complete list of ...
- Additional examples are given in the bibliography.
- Examples of ... were given above.

## Examples from interviews and quotations

When the supporting evidence is qualitative - direct speech from interviewees or quoted text - signal that an illustrative quotation or response is being introduced. Keep the framing neutral and attribute the source.

- In interviews, respondents stated that ...
- Typical responses were ...
- Typical statements were ...
- Typical comments by interviewees were ...
- ..., as mentioned by several respondents.
- The following quote illustrates ...
- As one interviewee stated, ...
- As seen in the following quotation by X, ...
- X states, for example, that ...
- This was expressed by one respondent as ...

## Counter-examples and limits of examples

Examples that contradict a generalization are as important as supporting ones. Frame them explicitly.

- A notable counter-example is ...
- One important counter-example to this generalization is ...
- The case of X, however, complicates this picture.
- This pattern is not, however, universal: the case of X shows that ...
- One exception to this generalization is ...
- An apparent counter-example is offered by ...
- The evidence is not, however, uniformly supportive: in the case of X, ...
- It should be noted that not all cases conform to this pattern. Author (Year), for example, reports ...

## Quantifying example coverage

State explicitly how representative the example is. Failing to do so is a common Q1 reviewer complaint.

- The example presented here is representative of the broader sample.
- This case is typical of the cohort.
- This case is atypical, but illustrative of the upper bound of ...
- The example given here was selected because it presents the clearest instance of ...
- The example below was chosen at random from the dataset.
- This is one of N similar cases in the dataset.
- Similar patterns were observed in M of the N cases examined.

## Sequencing multiple examples

When stacking examples, signal their order and their cumulative effect.

- A first example is ...
- A further example, drawn from a quite different context, is ...
- A more striking instance is ...
- Beyond these, a third case is worth considering: ...
- The examples above all share the feature of ...; the next, by contrast, ...
- Taken together, these examples suggest that ...
- These three cases, drawn from different periods / regions / disciplines, converge on the same observation: ...

## Common pitfalls

- **Stacking examples without analysis.** A list of three examples followed by nothing reads as an undigested data dump. Always close with a sentence that states what the examples collectively show.
- **Treating an example as proof.** Examples illustrate; they rarely prove. Hedge the inferential step: *suggests*, *is consistent with*, *illustrates the pattern that...*.
- **Cherry-picking unacknowledged.** If an example is chosen because it is unusually clear, say so; do not present it as typical when it is not.
- **Anecdote masquerading as evidence.** *"The evidence for X is purely anecdotal"* is sometimes a valid characterization of another author's work - and a fair warning when it applies to your own.
