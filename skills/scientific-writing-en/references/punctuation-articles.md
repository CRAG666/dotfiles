# Punctuation and article use

Mechanical rules that keep academic prose clear and unambiguous. Use this file when editing for correctness, not for phrasing. (For the ban on decorative Unicode punctuation, see principle 11 in SKILL.md: use straight quotes and a spaced hyphen, never curly quotes or the em/en dash glyph.)

## Punctuation

### Period (.)

- Ends a sentence.
- Marks an abbreviation: etc., et al., e.g., i.e. (some journals omit the stops; be consistent).
- Marks an omission in a quotation, shown as three spaced periods in brackets: [ ... ].

### Comma (,)

- Joins two independent clauses together with a coordinator (and, but, or, so): "The samples were prepared, and the assay was run the next day."
- Separates a leading dependent clause from the main clause: "Although the effect was small, it was consistent." (The comma is usually dropped when the dependent clause comes second.)
- Encloses additional, non-essential information: "The result, which we had not predicted, was robust."
- Marks a non-defining relative clause (adds information) but NOT a defining one (identifies which): non-defining "The reagent, which was new, failed." vs defining "The reagent that we ordered failed."
- Separates items in a list: "apples, oranges, and pears." US academic style uses the serial comma (the comma before the final "and"); the major US style guides (Chicago, APA, MLA) require it, so keep it consistently.
- Do NOT join two independent clauses with a comma alone (comma splice). Use a coordinator, a semicolon, or a period. Wrong: "The effect was significant, the sample was small." Right: "The effect was significant; the sample was small."

### Semicolon (;)

- Links two closely related independent clauses in place of a period: "Some prefer essays; others prefer presentations."
- Separates items in a grammatically complex list whose items already contain commas: "Motion is of four kinds: (1) motion which...; (2) motion which...; and (3) motion which..."

### Colon (:)

- Introduces an explanation or expansion of the preceding clause: "The reason was obvious: the equipment was faulty." (The part before the colon should be a complete clause.)
- Introduces a list, especially a complex one.
- Introduces a direct quotation, particularly a longer one: "Jones (2003) states that: '...'."

### Quotation marks (' ' or " ")

- Enclose a direct quotation.
- Flag a word used in a special or unusual sense (use sparingly).
- Use double quotation marks as the primary style; use single quotation marks only for a quotation inside a quotation.
- US convention: place commas and periods inside the closing quotation mark ("the so-called 'control,'"); place colons and semicolons outside.

### Parentheses ( ) and brackets [ ]

- Round parentheses enclose incidental information, an aside, or a citation.
- Square brackets enclose editorial insertions inside a quotation: "[emphasis added]", "[sic]", or an omission "[ ... ]".

### Dash and hyphen

- Avoid the em dash and en dash in formal academic writing. Replace with a colon, a semicolon, parentheses, or a spaced hyphen as appropriate.
- Use the hyphen for compound modifiers before a noun (well-known result, long-term effect, two-stage process) and for some prefixes (non-invasive, co-author, self-report); check the journal's preference for closed vs hyphenated forms.

### Apostrophe (')

- Marks the possessive: the study's aim, the participants' responses (plural possessive after the s).
- Do NOT use it for plurals (the 1990s, several PhDs) or in "its" (possessive) versus "it's" (= it is; avoided in formal prose anyway).

### Question mark and exclamation mark

- The exclamation mark has essentially no place in academic body prose; remove it. The question mark is used only for genuine questions (a listed research question), not for rhetorical questions inside paragraphs (see `academic-style.md`).

## Abbreviations and acronyms

- **Define on first use** - spell out the term with the abbreviation in parentheses the first time it appears, or list it in a table of abbreviations. Even standard abbreviations may be opaque to readers in an adjacent subfield, so do not assume. Define abbreviations used in tables and figures in the legend as well.
- **Periods (US) vs none (GB).** US English uses more stops (*Ph.D., U.S., Dr., Dept.*) than British (*PhD, UK, Dr, Dept*); some US authorities also drop them. Acronyms, chemical elements and units take no periods in either variety: *AIDS, DNA, Fe, Cu, kg, Hz*. Be consistent.
- **Plurals take no apostrophe:** *NGOs, MPs, CEOs* (not *NGO's*). A few scholarly abbreviations pluralize by doubling the letter: *p./pp.* (page/pages), *l./ll.* (line/lines), *f./ff.* (following), *MS/MSS* (manuscript/manuscripts), *sp./spp.* (species).
- **Do not pluralize unit symbols:** *50 cm, 90 kg* (not *cms, kgs*). See `grammar-numbers-units.md`.
- **Possessive uses the apostrophe normally:** *the UN's role*, *MEPs' expenses*.
- **US state / Canadian province abbreviations** appear in reference lists for publishers and equipment makers. Use either the two-letter postal codes (*CA, MA, DC; ON, BC, QC*) or the traditional forms (*Calif., Mass., D.C.; Ont., B.C., Que.*) consistently throughout - do not mix the two systems.

## Capitalization

- **Specific vs general.** Capitalize the name or title of a specific person, place or thing; use lower case for general terms. *the University of Manchester* but *a university*; *the Linguistics Department* but *a department of linguistics*; *Professor Smith* but *a professor*. Where a field allows either heavier or lighter capitalization, pick one and keep a style sheet for consistency.
- **Parts of a work.** Decide once whether cross-references are capitalized (*see Chapter 1, Table 4.5, Figure 6*) or not (*see chapter 1, table 4.5*), and follow the target journal's convention.
- **Titles.** Either title case (capitalize all main words: *A Vindication of the Rights of Woman*) or sentence case (capitalize only the first word and proper nouns: *A vindication of the rights of woman*). In title case, do not capitalize minor words unless first: articles (*a, an, the*), coordinators (*and, but, or, nor, if*), short prepositions (*in, on, of, to, at, for, with, by, about*), and *is/are*. A common reference style uses title case for book and journal titles but sentence case for article titles - be consistent.

## Punctuating titles, legends and bullets

- Headings and figure/table titles usually take no closing period. Table titles conventionally sit above the table, figure captions below the figure.
- In a bulleted or numbered list, choose one style: either each item is a fragment with no terminal punctuation, or each is a full sentence ending in a period. Do not mix. Bullets are acceptable only in genuine enumerations, not inside body paragraphs (SKILL.md principle 7).

## Article use (a / an / the / zero)

Article use is complex, but a few rules resolve most cases.

### Singular countable nouns

Always take a determiner, usually an article. Nouns that recur in academic writing and often trip up non-native writers: system, model, method, approach, group, problem, effect, level, investigation, study, participant, condition, category, sector. A determiner is still required even with an adjective or attributive noun in front:

- the greenhouse effect, the transport system, the control group
- a high level, a systematic approach, a rigorous study, an exploratory investigation

Use "a/an" to introduce something for the first time or as one of a class; use "the" once it is specific or already identified.

### Plural countable nouns

- Use "the" when referring to a specific, identified group: "The books in this collection were published in the 19th century."
- Use no article (zero) for a general reference: "Learners remember facts better when they are contextualized."

### Uncountable nouns

- Normally take no article: "Science is a systematic approach to answering questions." "Reliability is an important quality of any test."
- Take "the" when post-modified by "of..." or a defining relative clause: "The science of global warming is contested." "The reliability of this instrument is poor."

### Names and titles

- Normally no "the": Manchester University, Harvard, Nature.
- Use "the" when the name contains a post-modifying "of...": the University of Manchester, the United States of America.
- Use "the" with names containing organization / association / institute / society: the World Health Organization, the American Heart Association, the Royal Society.

### General strategy

Beyond these rules, notice how noun phrases are used in the published papers you read in your field, and check the source when unsure. Article choice is often field- and phrase-specific.
