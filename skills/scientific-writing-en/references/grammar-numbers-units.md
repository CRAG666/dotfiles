# Grammar, numbers and units

Mechanical correctness for the parts of academic English that spellcheckers miss: irregular plurals, subject-verb agreement on borrowed nouns, noncount nouns, number style, and SI/units. Use this file when editing for correctness, not for phrasing. (For punctuation, capitalization and abbreviations, see `punctuation-articles.md`; for spelling and confusing words, see `academic-style.md`.)

Keep output 7-bit ASCII (SKILL.md principle 11): write *degrees C* not the degree glyph, *m2* not superscript two, *1/2* not the vulgar-fraction glyph, *micro (u)* for the SI micro symbol.

## Irregular plurals

Words borrowed from Latin, Greek and French often keep a foreign plural. Some now also take a regular *-s*; preference varies by field and by formal vs informal register. When both are listed, the foreign form is usually the more formal and is expected in most science writing. Verify in the journal's house style.

| Pattern | Singular -> plural (examples) |
|---|---|
| -is -> -es | analysis -> analyses; axis -> axes; basis -> bases; crisis -> crises; diagnosis -> diagnoses; hypothesis -> hypotheses; parenthesis -> parentheses; synopsis -> synopses; thesis -> theses |
| -on -> -a | criterion -> criteria; phenomenon -> phenomena; ganglion -> ganglia; mitochondrion -> mitochondria; taxon -> taxa |
| -um -> -a | datum -> data; bacterium -> bacteria; medium -> media; stratum -> strata; addendum -> addenda; erratum -> errata; ovum -> ova; phylum -> phyla; spectrum -> spectra; symposium -> symposia (also symposiums); curriculum -> curricula (also curriculums); millennium -> millennia |
| -us -> -i | stimulus -> stimuli; nucleus -> nuclei (also nucleuses); radius -> radii; locus -> loci; fungus -> fungi; bacillus -> bacilli; syllabus -> syllabuses (also syllabi) |
| -us -> -ra | corpus -> corpora; genus -> genera |
| -x -> -ces | appendix -> appendices (also appendixes); index -> indices (also indexes); matrix -> matrices; vortex -> vortices; codex -> codices |
| -a -> -ae | formula -> formulae (also formulas); larva -> larvae; vertebra -> vertebrae; antenna -> antennae (also antennas) |
| -ma -> -mata | schema -> schemata (also schemas); stoma -> stomata; carcinoma -> carcinomas (also carcinomata); lymphoma -> lymphomas (also lymphomata) |
| unchanged | series -> series; species -> species |
| native irregulars | man -> men; woman -> women; child -> children; mouse -> mice; foot -> feet; tooth -> teeth |

Note: some *-o* and *-um* nouns take regular *-s* (embryos, neutrinos, placebos, museums, premiums). Do not over-correct; check the dictionary.

## Subject-verb agreement on borrowed nouns

The high-frequency trap in science writing is treating a foreign plural as singular. In formal academic prose, use the plural verb with the plural form:

- *The data are inconclusive.* (not *the data is*)
- *Several criteria were applied.* / *The sole criterion was X.*
- *These phenomena are well documented.* / *This phenomenon is rare.*
- *The media are often blamed.* / *The internet is a new medium.*
- *Some bacteria are harmful.* / *A single bacterium is too small to see.*
- *Sedimentary strata were deposited.* / *Each stratum represents a period.*

*Data* is increasingly used as a singular mass noun in informal and some technical registers, but Q1 journals in most fields still expect the plural; follow the target journal. *Bacteria, criteria, phenomena, media, strata* used as singular are errors in formal prose.

## Singular words ending in -s

Names of disciplines and diseases that look plural but take a singular verb:

- **Disciplines:** acoustics, dynamics, economics, electronics, ergonomics, ethics, genetics, hydraulics, linguistics, mathematics, optics, phonetics, physics, politics, robotics, semantics, semiotics, statistics. *Linguistics is the study of language. Statistics is a branch of mathematics.*
- **Diseases:** diabetes, measles, mumps, rabies, rickets, scabies, shingles. *Diabetes is a growing problem.*
- **Also:** news. *The news was a surprise.*

Exception: when the word denotes the *application* rather than the field, it can be plural - *the statistics were misleading*, *the acoustics of the hall are poor*.

## Noncount (uncountable) nouns

Several nouns common in research writing have no plural and usually take no *a/an*. Quantify them with a partitive phrase, not a number:

- research, evidence, information, knowledge, progress, equipment, software, education.
- Not: *a research, researches, an evidence, informations, equipments, softwares*.
- Use: *a piece of research/information/equipment*, *a great deal of evidence/knowledge*, *little progress*, *further research*, *the following software*.

(For article use with countable, uncountable and proper nouns, see `punctuation-articles.md`.)

## Numbers

### Words or figures

A common rule: spell out *one* through *ten* as words, use figures for larger numbers. Round/approximate numbers may be spelled out (*over fifty cases*, *around five thousand students*); exact values take figures (*54 new cases*, *5,023 students*). Always use figures with:

- units and symbols: *3%, 6 mm, 10 kg, 100 km, 25 degrees C, pH 7*
- page/figure/table numbers: *page 1, p. 100, Figure 6, Table 3*
- dates: *19 February 2008* (GB), *October 11, 2009* (US)

Follow the target journal's threshold; many science journals use figures for all numbers with units regardless of size.

### Sentence-initial numbers

Do not begin a sentence with a figure. Recast or spell it out:

- Not: *50% of students came from private schools.*
- Better: *Fifty percent of students came from private schools.* / *After 30 minutes, 2.5 mg was added.* (recast so the figure is not first)

### Mixing words and figures

Avoid mixing within a comparison: *from 5 to 16* (not *from five to 16*). But use a word + figure to prevent two adjacent numerals colliding: *three 5-point scales*, *five 10-year-old children*.

### Thousands, decimals and large values

- Mark thousands with a comma or a thin space, applied consistently: *29,483* or *29 483*; *6,728,349*. Numbers under 10,000 are often left unbroken (*3987* or *3,987*). A space avoids ambiguity because the comma is the decimal separator in many countries.
- The decimal separator in English is a **point**, never a comma: *0.5, 0.333, 0.25*. If your first language uses a decimal comma, check every figure - this is a frequent, serious error.
- Do **not** use a point to mark thousands (*3.500* reads as three-and-a-half in English).
- Do not add commas/spaces in page numbers: *page 1234*.
- Large round values, especially currency and populations, may mix figures and words: *3 million, USD 10 billion, around 60 million*.
- Do not pluralize exact multiples: *two hundred, three thousand, five billion* (not *hundreds/thousands* there). General multiples do take *-s*: *hundreds of researchers, thousands of cases*.
- *Billion* now normally means 10^9, but the older British sense of 10^12 occasionally survives; define it if ambiguity is possible.

### Percent and math symbols

- US *percent* (one word), British *per cent* (two words); be consistent. With a figure prefer the symbol: *10%*, with no space: *100%* (not *100 %*, not *ten %*).
- Space math operators consistently: *1 + 1 = 2*, *5 - 3 = 2*, *< 10*, *> 6*. Keep a sign attached to its number (*-45*, *10-2*) so it does not wrap to the next line.

### Roman numerals

Used for preliminary pages, occasionally chapters and list markers: i/I=1, v/V=5, x/X=10, l/L=50, c/C=100, d/D=500, m/M=1000. Add when a symbol of equal/greater value follows (XX=20, CX=110); subtract when a smaller value precedes a larger (IX=9, XC=90).

### Greek and Latin numerical affixes

Useful for parsing and coining technical terms: 1 mono-/uni-, 2 di-/bi-/du-, 3 tri-/ter-, 4 quadr-, 5 penta-/quin-, 6 hexa-/sex-, 7 sept-/hept-, 8 oct-, 9 non-/nona-, 10 dec-, 12 dodeca-, 100 cent-, 1000 milli-/mille-; half: semi-/demi-/hemi-; single: haplo-/mono-; double: diplo-/di-; first: proto-; few: oligo-; many: multi-/poly-.

## Units (SI)

Internationally and in research writing, SI units are the norm even where imperial units remain in everyday UK/US use.

### Formatting SI units and abbreviations

- Do not pluralize unit symbols: *50 cm, 90 kg* (not *cms, kgs*). See `punctuation-articles.md` on abbreviations.
- Put a (non-breaking) space between number and unit: *6 mm, 10 kg, 100 degrees C* - except *%* and *degrees of angle*, which close up (*360deg*, written as *360 degrees* in ASCII prose).
- Spelling differs GB/US: *metre/meter, litre/liter, gram, kilogram*. Use one consistently; *meter* in US English is also the device. Liter symbol is *l* or *L*.

### SI prefixes

Multiples: deca (da, 10), hecto (h, 100), kilo (k, 10^3), mega (M, 10^6), giga (G, 10^9), tera (T, 10^12), peta (P, 10^15), exa (E, 10^18), zetta (Z, 10^21), yotta (Y, 10^24).

Submultiples: deci (d, 10^-1), centi (c, 10^-2), milli (m, 10^-3), micro (u, 10^-6), nano (n, 10^-9), pico (p, 10^-12), femto (f, 10^-15), atto (a, 10^-18), zepto (z, 10^-21), yocto (y, 10^-24).

### Approximate SI / imperial equivalents

For readers who think in the other system: 1 cm ~ 0.39 in; 1 m ~ 3.28 ft ~ 1.09 yd; 1 km ~ 0.62 mile; 1 kg ~ 2.20 lb; 1 L ~ 0.26 US gal (0.22 GB gal); 1 ha ~ 2.47 acres. Reverse: 1 in = 2.54 cm; 1 ft = 30.5 cm; 1 mile = 1.609 km; 1 lb = 0.454 kg; 1 US gal = 3.785 L. Temperature: 32 F = 0 C, 212 F = 100 C. Note GB and US gallons, pints and fluid ounces differ - state which.
