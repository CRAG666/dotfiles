# Abstract and Conclusions

## Abstract structure (move template)

An abstract is a miniature of the whole paper. Build it from these moves, in this order. Aim and key findings are obligatory; the others are included as space and journal style allow (typically 150-300 words, one paragraph, no citations).

1. **Importance of the topic** - one sentence on why the area matters (draw on `introduction.md` Background/importance phrases). E.g. "X plays a central role in..." / "There is a recognized need for..."
2. **Reference to the current state of knowledge** - what is established (draw on `references-citations.md`). E.g. "A growing body of evidence suggests..." / "X has emerged as a key tool for..."
3. **The knowledge gap or problem** - what is missing (draw on `introduction.md` Knowledge gap). E.g. "However, X has yet to be..." / "Little is known about..."
4. **Aim / purpose of the study** (obligatory) - E.g. "This study examines / investigates / sets out to determine..."
5. **Methods used** - one or two sentences (draw on `methods.md`). E.g. "A mixed-method approach was used..." / "Data were collected from N participants via..."
6. **Key findings** (obligatory) - the main results, stated plainly (draw on `results.md`). E.g. "The results show that..." / "Analysis revealed that..."
7. **Implications / value** - the contribution or recommendation (draw on Suggesting implications below). E.g. "These findings suggest that..." / "This study contributes to..."

Tense guide: present perfect or past for what the study did and found ("This study investigated...", "The results showed..."); present for established knowledge and implications ("X is...", "These findings suggest...").
