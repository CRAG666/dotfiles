# Critical writing · Being critical

Being critical in academic writing means questioning what you read, identifying weaknesses in arguments or methods, and — crucially — pairing criticism with constructive suggestions and positive evaluation where warranted. A literature review that contains only criticism reads as polemical; one that contains only praise reads as uncritical. Q1 reviewers expect calibrated, evidence-grounded evaluation of every source cited.

## Evaluative adjectives for commenting on a piece of research

Pair an adjective with a precise noun (*analysis*, *examination*, *study*, *survey*, *investigation*, *account*, *treatment*, *synthesis*) rather than using bare praise like "excellent paper".

- Positive: excellent, comprehensive, detailed, useful, exhaustive, innovative, timely, important, impressive, influential, seminal, pioneering, thorough, rigorous, sophisticated, original, insightful, careful, robust, well-designed.
- Neutral / qualifying: limited, exploratory, small-scale, preliminary, descriptive, qualitative.
- Negative: superficial, narrow, methodologically weak, dated, biased, overstated, under-theorized, anecdotal, methodologically flawed, contested.

## Critical-paragraph template

A useful Q1-style critical paragraph has the following moves:

1. **Acknowledge** the source's contribution or position: *Author's study of X remains a benchmark for…*
2. **Identify** a specific limitation: *However, the analysis is restricted to a single cohort drawn from…*
3. **Substantiate** the limitation with evidence or reasoning: *This restriction means that the reported effect of Y may reflect …*
4. **Constructive turn**: *A study designed to address this limitation would need to…*
5. **Transition** to your own contribution: *The present work takes this step by…*

Avoid the inverse — opening with a blunt dismissal and only later acknowledging value. Reviewers read this as petulant.
