# Shared knowledge and signposting

Reviewers expect a Q1 paper to (1) flag when a claim is widely accepted in the field rather than the author's own, and (2) signpost the reader through the structure of the document. Both functions are heavily phraseological - this is exactly the territory where non-native and AI-generated prose tends to slip.

## Indicating shared knowledge or established understanding

Use these when the proposition is part of the accepted background of the discipline. Pair with a representative citation when possible; bare assertions of "as is well known" can read as bluster.

### Assuming shared understanding with the reader

- Of course, ...
- Clearly, ...
- It is clear that ...
- It is obvious that ...
- There is now no doubt that ...
- Given this situation, it is hardly surprising that ...
- One should not, of course, accept without question all ...

### Hedged shared-understanding frames

- It is, of course, true that ...
- It is, of course, difficult to ...
- It is, of course, possible that ...
- It is, of course, inevitable that ...
- It is, of course, recognized that ...
- It is often the case that ...
- It is debatable whether ...
- It is unrealistic to expect ...
- It is entirely possible that ...
- It is too early to say whether ...
- It is important to acknowledge ...
- It is important to be cautious about ...
- It is impossible to arrive at a reliable estimate of ...

### Endorsing an author's claim as obviously correct

- Author is clearly right to include ...
- Author is right to highlight ...
- Author is right to question ...
- Author is right to argue that ...
- Author is right to point out that ...
- Author is right to draw our attention to ...
- Author is right to stress the importance of ...

### Assuming shared knowledge with members of the discipline

- As is well known, ...
- As has been shown, ...
- As has been widely documented, ...
- It is well known that ...
- It is well established that ...
- It is widely accepted that ...
- It is widely acknowledged that ...
- It is generally understood that ...
- It is now generally accepted that ...
- It is common knowledge that X is ...
- It is well known from previous studies that ...
- There is now broad consensus that ...
- Author's well-known argument is that ...
- It is well known among Xs that ...
- What has been established and is now generally accepted is that ...
- There is now a relatively broad consensus across disciplines that ...

### Cautious framing of "established" claims

When a claim is widely held but not entirely settled:

- It is generally, though not universally, accepted that ...
- The standard view, with some dissent, is that ...
- A common, though contested, assumption is that ...
- The dominant interpretation has been that ...
- Until recently, the prevailing view was that ...
- Most accounts assume, with varying degrees of qualification, that ...

## Previewing a paper, chapter or section

## A note on signpost density

Q1 introductions and discussions are heavily signposted; methods and results sections less so (the structure is implicit in the IMRaD ordering). A 10 000-word thesis chapter typically contains a roadmap paragraph at the start, a brief summary at the end, and transition sentences at the boundary of every subsection. A 5 000-word article needs less - usually just one preview at the end of the introduction and brief topic sentences within each section.

Over-signposting reads as didactic; under-signposting leaves the reader lost. The right amount is determined by the genre, not by personal preference.
