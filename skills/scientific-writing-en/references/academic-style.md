# Academic style

This file collects style-level rules and substitutions that distinguish Q1-tier scientific prose from informal, journalistic, or AI-generated English. Apply these as a final-pass checklist on any draft.

## Eight defining features of academic style

1. **Evidence-based.** Every non-obvious claim is backed by a citation, a statistic, or an example. Assertions without support read as opinion.
2. **Words of classical origin.** Academic English favors Latinate vocabulary over native Anglo-Saxon equivalents (see substitution table below).
3. **Cautious.** Claims are calibrated to the strength of the evidence. *"Drinking alcohol causes breast cancer in women"* becomes *"Some studies suggest that drinking alcohol increases the risk of breast cancer in women."*
4. **Impersonal.** First-person *I* is avoided in most disciplines; first-person plural *we* is acceptable for multi-author empirical work in science and medicine. *You* is not used to address the reader.
5. **No contractions.** *Don't*, *isn't*, *it's*, *can't* are all written in full (*do not*, *is not*, *it is*, *cannot*). Exception: when quoting recorded speech.
6. **Nominalised.** Verbs are often transformed into noun phrases to pack information densely. ("The Liverpool–Manchester railway opened in 1830" → "The opening of the Liverpool–Manchester railway in 1830").
7. **No rhetorical questions in body prose.** *"Is the welfare system effective?"* becomes *"It is important to consider the effectiveness of the welfare system."* Listed research questions in an introduction are fine; rhetorical questions inside paragraphs are not.
8. **Precise and detailed.** Vague intensifiers (*very*, *really*, *extremely*) and approximate quantifiers (*a lot of*, *quite a few*, *kind of*) are replaced with specific values or precise adjectives.

## Vague intensifiers and hedges to cut or sharpen

The following adverbs and adjectives add length without precision. Either delete them or replace with a quantified term.

- very, really, extremely, absolutely, incredibly, totally, completely, utterly
- quite, rather, fairly, somewhat (acceptable as deliberate hedges, but not in every sentence)
- a lot, lots of, tons of
- kind of, sort of
- pretty (as in "pretty effective")

Replacements:

- "very important" → "central", "of central importance", "fundamental"
- "very large" → "substantial", "considerable" (or a number)
- "very different" → "markedly different", "qualitatively different"
- "very small" → "negligible", "minimal" (or a number)
- "really" → cut
- "extremely high" → quantify

## Reporting verbs by stance

Choose deliberately. Each verb signals your attitude toward the source.

- **Neutral reporting:** state, report, note, observe, find, write, describe, present.
- **Endorsing:** demonstrate, establish, confirm, prove, show (use sparingly), reveal.
- **Tentative / cautious:** suggest, indicate, imply, hint at, propose, posit.
- **Argumentative:** argue, contend, maintain, hold, claim, assert, insist.
- **Distancing / skeptical:** assert (with care), claim, allege.
- **Constructive / synthetic:** synthesize, integrate, draw together, frame, conceptualize.
- **Critical:** challenge, question, dispute, reject, refute, problematize.

## Personal pronouns: when *we* and *I* are acceptable

- *We* is standard in multi-author empirical papers in the sciences and medicine: *"We collected samples from 32 patients."*
- *I* is acceptable in single-author work in the humanities and in reflective sections (research positioning, motivation), but not in methods or results.
- *You* is not used in academic body prose.
- *One* is acceptable but increasingly old-fashioned in scientific writing — *we* or the passive is usually smoother.

In disciplines that prohibit first-person reference altogether, replace with impersonal constructions:

- "we measured X" → "X was measured"
- "we then conclude that" → "It can therefore be concluded that"
- "we found" → "the present study found", "the analysis revealed"

## Gender-neutral and bias-free language

- Replace *he* used generically with *they* (now widely accepted as singular), *he or she*, or recast in the plural.
- Avoid gendered occupational terms: *chairman* → *chair*; *fireman* → *firefighter*; *manpower* → *workforce*, *staffing*; *mankind* → *humanity*, *humankind*.
- Use *participants* rather than *subjects* in most human-research contexts (some disciplines still prefer *subjects*; follow the journal's convention).
- Refer to people by the term they use for themselves; check the current convention in the relevant clinical or social-science literature.

## Avoiding rhetorical questions in body prose

A rhetorical question in academic prose usually signals that the writer is unsure how to assert a claim. Convert to a statement.

- ✗ *Is X effective? The answer appears to be yes.*
- ✓ *The evidence reviewed below suggests that X is effective in most contexts.*
- ✗ *Why does X occur? Several explanations have been proposed.*
- ✓ *Several explanations for the occurrence of X have been proposed.*

Listed research questions in an introduction (e.g., "*RQ1: Does X affect Y?*") are fine, because they are framed as research questions, not as rhetorical moves.

## Common register slips that mark a draft as informal

- "*…this huge problem…*" → "*…this substantial / pressing / widespread problem…*"
- "*…basically what the data show is…*" → "*…the data show that…*"
- "*kind of supports…*" → "*lends some support to…*", "*is broadly consistent with…*"
- "*shows up*" → "*appears*", "*emerges*"
- "*came up with*" → "*proposed*", "*developed*", "*formulated*"
- "*got around*" → "*addressed*", "*circumvented*"
- "*on top of that…*" → "*in addition…*", "*moreover…*"
- "*at the end of the day…*" → cut, or "*ultimately*", "*on balance*"
- "*these days…*" → "*in recent years…*", "*at present…*"
- "*so-called*" → use only with deliberate scare-quote intent; otherwise cut.

## Padding to delete

These constructions add words without meaning:

- "in order to" → "to"
- "due to the fact that" → "because"
- "in spite of the fact that" → "although"
- "with regard to" / "in respect of" → "regarding", "for", or recast
- "for the purpose of" + gerund → infinitive ("for the purpose of measuring" → "to measure")
- "it is important to note that" → cut, or simply assert
- "it should be pointed out that" → cut
- "there are X studies that have shown…" → "X studies have shown…"
- "the fact that" → recast ("Despite the fact that X" → "Although X")
- "a large number of" → "many"
- "a small number of" → "few"
- "a wide range of" → "a range of", or be specific
- "in the event that" → "if"
- "at the present moment" → "now", "at present"

## Quantity expressions

Be precise. Vague quantity is the most common AI tell.

- ✗ *several*, *some*, *many*, *most*, *a few* — fine when truly approximate, but pair with a citation or count when possible.
- ✓ *N = 47*, *roughly two-thirds*, *fewer than 5%*, *between 1995 and 2010*.
- Use "*the majority of*" only when you mean a clear >50%. "*Many*" or "*most*" is often more honest.
- "*Significant*" should refer to statistical significance when used in a results section; replace with "*substantial*" or "*marked*" in discussion if you mean magnitude.

## A note on hedging without becoming evasive

Hedging is calibrated, not blanket. Avoid stacked hedges ("*it may possibly be the case that…*") — pick one hedge per claim.

- single hedge: *the data suggest…*, *one explanation is…*, *X may be…*
- stacked hedges (avoid): *it could possibly be the case that X might perhaps be…*

A confident assertion is appropriate when the evidence supports it. Under-claiming is just as bad as over-claiming.
