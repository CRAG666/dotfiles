# Academic style

This file collects style-level rules and substitutions that distinguish Q1-tier scientific prose from informal, journalistic, or AI-generated English. Apply these as a final-pass checklist on any draft.

## Eight defining features of academic style

1. **Evidence-based.** Every non-obvious claim is backed by a citation, a statistic, or an example. Assertions without support read as opinion.
2. **Words of classical origin.** Academic English favors Latinate vocabulary over native Anglo-Saxon equivalents (see substitution table below).
3. **Cautious.** Claims are calibrated to the strength of the evidence. *"Drinking alcohol causes breast cancer in women"* becomes *"Some studies suggest that drinking alcohol increases the risk of breast cancer in women."*
4. **Impersonal.** First-person *I* is avoided in most disciplines; first-person plural *we* is acceptable for multi-author empirical work in science and medicine. *You* is not used to address the reader.
5. **No contractions.** *Don't*, *isn't*, *it's*, *can't* are all written in full (*do not*, *is not*, *it is*, *cannot*). Exception: when quoting recorded speech.
6. **Nominalized.** Verbs are often transformed into noun phrases to pack information densely. ("The transcontinental railroad opened in 1869" -> "The opening of the transcontinental railroad in 1869").
7. **No rhetorical questions in body prose.** *"Is the welfare system effective?"* becomes *"It is important to consider the effectiveness of the welfare system."* Listed research questions in an introduction are fine; rhetorical questions inside paragraphs are not.
8. **Precise and detailed.** Vague intensifiers (*very*, *really*, *extremely*) and approximate quantifiers (*a lot of*, *quite a few*, *kind of*) are replaced with specific values or precise adjectives.

## Clichés: overused phrases to avoid

A cliché is an overused, formulaic phrase. In academic prose it reads as filler and can devalue otherwise rigorous work. Replace with a literal, specific statement. Common offenders:

- *at the end of the day* -> ultimately, on balance, in the final analysis
- *the bottom line* -> in short, the key finding is, the central point is
- *a whole new ball game* -> fundamentally different, a qualitative shift
- *it is not rocket science* -> cut; state the point plainly
- *in this day and age* -> at present, in recent years, currently
- *the name of the game* -> the central concern, the determining factor
- *in my humble opinion* -> cut (and avoid first-person opinion framing)
- *guesstimate* -> estimate, approximation
- *24/7* -> continuously, around the clock
- *better late than never*, *over the moon*, *the be-all and end-all*, *you name it* -> cut and rephrase literally

This is a guideline, not an absolute rule: a vivid phrase can occasionally work as a deliberately chosen title, but never as default body prose.

## Vague intensifiers and hedges to cut or sharpen

The following adverbs and adjectives add length without precision. Either delete them or replace with a quantified term.

- very, really, extremely, absolutely, incredibly, totally, completely, utterly
- quite, rather, fairly, somewhat (acceptable as deliberate hedges, but not in every sentence)
- a lot, lots of, tons of
- kind of, sort of
- pretty (as in "pretty effective")

Replacements:

- "very important" -> "central", "of central importance", "fundamental"
- "very large" -> "substantial", "considerable" (or a number)
- "very different" -> "markedly different", "qualitatively different"
- "very small" -> "negligible", "minimal" (or a number)
- "really" -> cut
- "extremely high" -> quantify

## Reporting verbs by stance

Choose deliberately. Each verb signals your attitude toward the source.

- **Neutral reporting:** state, report, note, observe, find, write, describe, present.
- **Endorsing:** demonstrate, establish, confirm, prove, show (use sparingly), reveal.
- **Tentative / cautious:** suggest, indicate, imply, hint at, propose, posit.
- **Argumentative:** argue, contend, maintain, hold, claim, assert, insist.
- **Distancing / skeptical:** assert (with care), claim, allege.
- **Constructive / synthetic:** synthesize, integrate, draw together, frame, conceptualize.
- **Critical:** challenge, question, dispute, reject, refute, problematize.

## Personal pronouns: when *we* and *I* are acceptable

- *We* is standard in multi-author empirical papers in the sciences and medicine: *"We collected samples from 32 patients."*
- *I* is acceptable in single-author work in the humanities and in reflective sections (research positioning, motivation), but not in methods or results.
- *You* is not used in academic body prose.
- *One* is acceptable but increasingly old-fashioned in scientific writing - *we* or the passive is usually smoother.

In disciplines that prohibit first-person reference altogether, replace with impersonal constructions:

- "we measured X" -> "X was measured"
- "we then conclude that" -> "It can therefore be concluded that"
- "we found" -> "the present study found", "the analysis revealed"

## Gender-neutral and bias-free language

- Replace *he* used generically with *they* (now widely accepted as singular), *he or she*, or recast in the plural.
- Avoid gendered occupational terms: *chairman* -> *chair*; *fireman* -> *firefighter*; *manpower* -> *workforce*, *staffing*; *mankind* -> *humanity*, *humankind*.
- Use *participants* rather than *subjects* in most human-research contexts (some disciplines still prefer *subjects*; follow the journal's convention).
- Refer to people by the term they use for themselves; check the current convention in the relevant clinical or social-science literature.

**Test for bias by reversal.** Swap the group, sex, or pronoun in a statement and reread it. If the reversed version reads as loaded or unfair, the original carries bias. Likewise, beware false-consensus framings that presume the reader shares your background: *"As we all know..."*, *"as everyone knows..."* - cut these and support the claim instead.

## Cultural, socioeconomic, and regional bias

Bias often arises from treating one's own group as the unmarked norm and others as deviations from it. This is easy to miss and frequently invisible to the writer.

- **Loaded socioeconomic / development labels.** *developing countries*, *Third World*, *underdeveloped*, *upper / lower class* carry judgment. Prefer neutral, specific terms: *industrialized countries*, *low-* and *middle-income countries* (or name the specific countries / use the World Bank income classification the field uses).
- **Avoid parochialism for an international audience.** *Western countries* (as a stand-in for "advanced") -> *industrialized countries*. Name institutions in full and locate them: *Congress* -> *the US Congress*; *the EPA* -> *the US Environmental Protection Agency*.
- **Ambiguous continental labels.** Do not use *America / American(s)* to mean the United States when ambiguity is possible; write *the United States* / *U.S. residents*.
- **"Anglo-Saxon"** as shorthand for English-speaking countries is both inaccurate and potentially offensive; write *English-speaking countries*.
- **Seasons are hemisphere-dependent.** For an international readership, give months or dates rather than *spring 2024* / *summer 2024*.
- **Currency ambiguity.** Many countries use the same currency name (dollar, peso, pound). Where ambiguity is possible, use the ISO code: *USD 100,000*, *AUD 50,000*.
- **Era / dating symbols.** The asterisk (for born) and dagger (U+2020, for died) symbols carry Christian connotation; in cross-cultural writing prefer *b.* and *d.* (e.g., *Charles Darwin (b. 1809; d. 1882)*).

## Avoiding rhetorical questions in body prose

A rhetorical question in academic prose usually signals that the writer is unsure how to assert a claim. Convert to a statement.

- Avoid: *Is X effective? The answer appears to be yes.*
- Prefer: *The evidence reviewed below suggests that X is effective in most contexts.*
- Avoid: *Why does X occur? Several explanations have been proposed.*
- Prefer: *Several explanations for the occurrence of X have been proposed.*

Listed research questions in an introduction (e.g., "*RQ1: Does X affect Y?*") are fine, because they are framed as research questions, not as rhetorical moves.

## Common register slips that mark a draft as informal

- "*...this huge problem...*" -> "*...this substantial / pressing / widespread problem...*"
- "*...basically what the data show is...*" -> "*...the data show that...*"
- "*kind of supports...*" -> "*lends some support to...*", "*is broadly consistent with...*"
- "*shows up*" -> "*appears*", "*emerges*"
- "*came up with*" -> "*proposed*", "*developed*", "*formulated*"
- "*got around*" -> "*addressed*", "*circumvented*"
- "*on top of that...*" -> "*in addition...*", "*moreover...*"
- "*at the end of the day...*" -> cut, or "*ultimately*", "*on balance*"
- "*these days...*" -> "*in recent years...*", "*at present...*"
- "*so-called*" -> use only with deliberate scare-quote intent; otherwise cut.

## Padding to delete

These constructions add words without meaning:

- "in order to" -> "to"
- "due to the fact that" -> "because"
- "in spite of the fact that" -> "although"
- "with regard to" / "in respect of" -> "regarding", "for", or recast
- "for the purpose of" + gerund -> infinitive ("for the purpose of measuring" -> "to measure")
- "it is important to note that" -> cut, or simply assert
- "it should be pointed out that" -> cut
- "there are X studies that have shown..." -> "X studies have shown..."
- "the fact that" -> recast ("Despite the fact that X" -> "Although X")
- "a large number of" -> "many"
- "a small number of" -> "few"
- "a wide range of" -> "a range of", or be specific
- "in the event that" -> "if"
- "at the present moment" -> "now", "at present"

## Quantity expressions

Be precise. Vague quantity is the most common AI tell.

- Avoid: *several*, *some*, *many*, *most*, *a few* - fine when truly approximate, but pair with a citation or count when possible.
- Prefer: *N = 47*, *roughly two-thirds*, *fewer than 5%*, *between 1995 and 2010*.
- Use "*the majority of*" only when you mean a clear >50%. "*Many*" or "*most*" is often more honest.
- "*Significant*" should refer to statistical significance when used in a results section; replace with "*substantial*" or "*marked*" in discussion if you mean magnitude.

## A note on hedging without becoming evasive

Hedging is calibrated, not blanket. Avoid stacked hedges ("*it may possibly be the case that...*") - pick one hedge per claim.

- single hedge: *the data suggest...*, *one explanation is...*, *X may be...*
- stacked hedges (avoid): *it could possibly be the case that X might perhaps be...*

A confident assertion is appropriate when the evidence supports it. Under-claiming is just as bad as over-claiming.

## Nominalization: use it, but do not overload

Turning verbs into noun phrases packs information densely and is a hallmark of scientific register (*"the railroad opened"* -> *"the opening of the railroad"*). But chains of nominalizations make sentences hard to parse:

- Avoid: *the effect of the reduction of aggressiveness by the production of an ACTH-mediated condition of decreased androgen levels*
- Prefer: recast with a verb or two as the engine of the sentence: *reducing androgen levels via an ACTH-mediated mechanism lowered aggressiveness*

Rule of thumb: one or two nominalizations per sentence sharpens; three or more usually signals that a buried verb should be released. If a sentence has no finite verb carrying real meaning, un-nominalize.

## Paragraph development: controlling idea then support

A well-formed academic paragraph states one controlling idea (the topic sentence, in general terms) and then develops it with supporting material - and nothing else.

- Open with the new point in general terms.
- Follow with support: statistics, examples, a citation to prior research, a reason or explanation, a consequence, or specific detail.
- Every supporting sentence must relate back to the topic sentence; a genuinely new point belongs in its own paragraph.
- Do not merely restate the topic sentence in different words - support it.
- **Link sentences thematically.** Each sentence should pick up an idea introduced in the previous one (via a referring phrase, a repeated key term, or a connective), so the paragraph reads as a chain rather than a list. Example chain: *"...competitive sport at early ages. Early involvement ... exposes individuals to stress ... Among cyclists the most serious of these disorders is ..."* - the bold links carry the reader forward.

## Sentence-structure errors to eliminate

- **Sentence fragments.** A dependent clause is not a sentence. Avoid: *"Although a number of studies have been undertaken."* / *"Whereas literate societies have a definite awareness of the past."* Attach it to its main clause.
- **Comma splices / run-ons.** Two independent clauses cannot be joined by a comma alone. Avoid: *"Something is lost when people become literate, they consider it worth losing."* Fix with a coordinator (*and, but, so*), a semicolon, or a period.
- **Dashes in formal prose.** The em dash is acceptable but easily overused; where a colon, semicolon, or parentheses would do the work, prefer those for a more formal register.

## Commonly confused word pairs

Spellcheckers do not catch a correctly spelled wrong word. Verify these by meaning:

- **affect / effect** - *affect* is the verb (*A affects B*); *effect* is the noun (*the greenhouse effect*), used after a determiner. (*Effect* as a verb means "to bring about.")
- **comprise / consist** - both mean "to be made up of," but only *consist* takes *of* (*consists of three parts*; *comprises three parts*).
- **discrete / discreet** - *discrete* = separate, distinct; *discreet* = tactful, unobtrusive.
- **principle / principal** - *principle* (noun) = a rule or tenet; *principal* (adjective) = main, chief.
- **precede / proceed** - *precede* = come before; *proceed* = go forward, carry on.
- **formerly / formally** - *formerly* = earlier, previously; *formally* = officially, conventionally.
- **later / latter** - *later* (adverb) = at a subsequent time; *latter* (adjective) = the last-mentioned of two.
- **its / it's** - *its* = possessive; *it's* = "it is"/"it has" (and a contraction, so avoided in academic prose anyway).
- **i.e. / e.g.** - *i.e.* (id est) = "that is," "in other words"; *e.g.* (exempli gratia) = "for example."
- **prescribe / proscribe** - *prescribe* = recommend or authorize; *proscribe* = forbid, prohibit.
